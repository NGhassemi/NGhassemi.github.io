From Dissociative Identity Disorder, to Schizophrenia, Marvel has long been inclusive of
mental disorders. “The [schizophrenic] lives in the midst of affects that are not canalized
and regulated by social codifications … Furthermore, the schizophrenic’s perception would
be differential and thus fragmented” (der Wielen). Among the most famous and well-loved
hero’s, Wade Wilson, aka Deadpool, is famous for his goofy nature and being self-aware.
Even without that academic definition of schizophrenia, Deadpool is canonically schizophrenic,
having developed it due to his extreme healing abilities. Deadpool also displays character tendencies
that are well out of being regulated and canalized by society. For instance, he has no remorse in killing
his enemies, where most of the other Avengers will not kill. Deadpool also stands a unique intersection
between disability and mental disorder. Wade Wilson, the man underneath the Deadpool mask was ironically,
horribly, and permanently disfigured when he acquired his healing powers in an effort to cure his terminal cancer.
