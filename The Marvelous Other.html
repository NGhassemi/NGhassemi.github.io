The Marvelous Other

The superhero genre is generally ridiculed. Everyone from big name film directors, to literary
critics and intellectual filmgoers turn up their noses at the thought of a superhero film or comic book.
Most recently, a renowned film director stated that the superhero genre (namely the Marvel Universe)
was merely a “theme park”, something you go to solely for amusement. While these comics and movies
are indeed entertaining, they are so much more. It is because of their entertainment value that audiences
are more ready to accept their interwoven messages. For instance, during the racial unrest of the ‘60’s,
Stan Lee and Jack Kirby created Black Panther, the first African superhero. The Black Panther series
was hugely important as a “powerful source of racial meaning, narrative, and narrative in American
society that expresses a myriad of racial assumptions, political perspectives and (re)imaginings of black
identity” (Nama 4). The ability to create a narrative that spoke against stereotyping the other is also
important for those suffering from a disability or mental illness. Marvel has been inclusive almost
since its inception. Daredevil, the blind crime fighting lawyer from New York for example, was
introduced in 1964, only 25 years after the studio was created. What this exhibit hopes to accomplish is
to display that the superhero genre is not only meant to be a theme park filled with various
roller coasters for amusement. It examines the importance of creating the “superhero like me”.
